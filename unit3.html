Unit Overview

In this 45-day unit, students quantify energy changes in physical and chemical processes, perform calorimetry, construct and interpret reaction energy diagrams, and analyze factors affecting reaction rate. Students connect energy accounting to particle-level models and design strategies to change rates and yields under realistic constraints.

Standards for Unit 3:  HS-PS3-1 HS-PS3-4 HS-PS1-4 HS-PS1-5 HS-PS1-6

Essential Questions

  1. EQ1: How is energy stored, transferred, and measured in physical changes and chemical reactions?
  2. EQ2: What controls how fast reactions happen, and how can we change conditions to meet real-world goals?

Week 1 (Days 1–5) — Energy Concepts & Measurement

Focus Energy forms, conservation, system vs surroundings, temperature vs heat, specific heat.

Day 1 — Energy & Systems

Objective: Distinguish system/surroundings; identify energy transfers (heat/work) qualitatively.

In class: PhET demo (Energy Forms & Changes); energy flow diagrams.

Homework: Reading notes on energy forms and conservation.

Checkpoint: Exit ticket — label energy in a melting ice scenario.

Day 2 — Temperature vs Heat

Objective: Explain temperature as average kinetic energy; heat as energy transfer.

In class: Particulate model animations; quick demos with metal/wood blocks.

Homework: Concept practice (temp vs heat).

Checkpoint: Quick check — identify heat vs temperature in examples.

Day 3 — Specific Heat Capacity

Objective: Use q = m·c·ΔT for heating/cooling calculations.

In class: Worked examples; unit analysis; estimation strategies.

Homework: Specific heat practice set.

Checkpoint: Exit ticket — solve a q=mcΔT problem.

Day 4 — Calorimetry (Concepts)

Objective: Describe coffee-cup calorimetry assumptions; set up energy balance.

In class: Sample calorimetry data; discuss error sources and insulation.

Homework: Pre-lab questions for calorimetry lab.

Checkpoint: Pre-lab check — identify system/surroundings in lab.

Day 5 — Lab: Coffee-Cup Calorimetry (Specific Heat of Metal)

Objective: Measure c of a metal via hot metal into cool water method.

In class: Run trials; compute qwater and infer qmetal (−qwater).

Homework: Finish calculations; percent error.

Checkpoint: Lab check — c within reasonable range.

Week 2 (Days 6–10) — Enthalpy & Phase Changes

Focus Endothermic/exothermic, heating/cooling curves, phase change energy, calorimetry practice.

Day 6 — Endo vs Exothermic

Objective: Identify sign of q for system; draw energy flow arrows.

In class: Hand warmer vs cold pack analysis; particle-level sketches.

Homework: Endo/exo practice problems.

Checkpoint: Exit ticket — classify scenarios as endo/exo.

Day 7 — Heating/Cooling Curves

Objective: Interpret plateaus; calculate energy for temperature change vs phase change.

In class: Graph analysis; piecewise q calculations (q = m·c·ΔT; q = m·ΔHphase).

Homework: Heating curve practice set.

Checkpoint: Quick check — energy for melting step.

Day 8 — Calorimetry Practice (Solutions)

Objective: Calculate ΔHrxn from temperature change of solution (q = m·c·ΔT).

In class: Worked examples; assumptions (c, density); sign conventions.

Homework: Calorimetry problem set.

Checkpoint: Exit ticket — sign of ΔH for observed T change.

Day 9 — Lab: Dissolution/Neutralization Calorimetry

Objective: Measure ΔH for a solution process (e.g., NaOH(aq) neutralization) or salt dissolution.

In class: Run calorimetry; compute ΔH per mole; discuss assumptions.

Homework: Finish analysis; error discussion.

Checkpoint: Lab check — ΔH reported with sig figs & units.

Day 10 — Formative Quiz & Reteach

Objective: Assess energy skills; targeted reteach.

In class: Short quiz (q=mcΔT, heating curves, signs); small-group support.

Homework: Corrections/extra practice.

Checkpoint: Formal formative — quiz.

Week 3 (Days 11–15) — Reaction Enthalpy & Energy Diagrams

Focus ΔHrxn, Hess’s Law concepts (qualitative), reaction coordinate diagrams, activation energy & catalysts.

Day 11 — Reaction Enthalpy (ΔHrxn)

Objective: Define ΔHrxn; connect sign to endo/exo reactions.

In class: Example reactions; energy accounting with surroundings.

Homework: ΔH problems (no bond enthalpy tables needed yet).

Checkpoint: Exit ticket — classify reaction by sign of ΔH.

Day 12 — Energy Diagrams

Objective: Interpret reaction coordinate diagrams; identify Ea, ΔH, activated complex.

In class: Sketch/label diagrams for exo vs endo; compare heights.

Homework: Diagram labeling practice.

Checkpoint: Quick check — identify Ea on a diagram.

Day 13 — Catalysts & Energy Barriers

Objective: Explain how catalysts lower Ea and speed up reactions without changing ΔH.

In class: Enzyme and industrial catalysts examples; diagram comparisons.

Homework: Catalyst reading & questions.

Checkpoint: Exit ticket — does a catalyst change ΔH? Explain.

Day 14 — Lab: Energy Diagram from Data (Option 3A)

Objective: Collect temperature vs time data for an exo/endo process and sketch a qualitative energy diagram.

In class: Measure T(t) during dissolution/neutralization; infer ΔH sign and relative Ea with/without catalyst.

Homework: Complete graph & brief explanation.

Checkpoint: Lab check — diagram correctly annotated.

Day 15 — Alternative Lab: Arrhenius Demonstration (Option 3B)

Objective: Observe temperature dependence of rate (e.g., iodine clock variants) and relate to energy barrier.

In class: Compare cold vs warm conditions; discuss effective collisions.

Homework: Short write-up linking observations to Ea.

Checkpoint: Exit ticket — describe how T affected rate.

Week 4 (Days 16–20) — Collision Theory & Rate Laws (Qualitative)

Focus Effective collisions, concentration/temperature/surface area, catalysts; initial-rate thinking qualitatively.

Day 16 — Collision Theory

Objective: Describe effective collisions (orientation & energy threshold).

In class: PhET Collision Lab; model-based explanations.

Homework: Concept practice.

Checkpoint: Exit ticket — what makes a collision effective?

Day 17 — Concentration & Rate

Objective: Explain how higher concentration increases collision frequency, thus rate.

In class: Stations comparing rate at different [A]; graphical reasoning.

Homework: Qualitative rate problems.

Checkpoint: Quick check — predict rate change when [A] doubles.

Day 18 — Temperature & Rate

Objective: Relate higher T to increased fraction of effective collisions.

In class: Arrhenius rationale (qualitative); hot vs cold demo.

Homework: Write short mechanistic explanation.

Checkpoint: Exit ticket — explain T effect in one sentence.

Day 19 — Surface Area & Catalysts

Objective: Describe how surface area/catalysts change rate without changing ΔH.

In class: Alka-Seltzer whole vs crushed; MnO₂-catalyzed H2O2 demo.

Homework: Catalyst case study (industrial or biological).

Checkpoint: Quick quiz — choose best way to speed up a reaction.

Day 20 — Formative Quiz & Reteach

Homework: Corrections; choice practice.

Checkpoint: Formal formative — quiz.

Problem-Based Assignment: “Hot Pack, Cold Pack, or Fast Track?”
Teams receive a client scenario with target performance (temperature/time window, safety & cost constraints). They must select conditions and justify them using energy calculations (q = m·c·ΔT; heating/phase steps) and rate reasoning (T, [ ], surface area, catalysts). Deliverable: a brief solution with data, graphs, calculations, and a justified recommendation (ties to HS-PS3-1, HS-PS1-5, HS-PS3-4, HS-PS1-6).
